Framing

Improve your relationship to sensations

It’s that time again. Whether it’s post-holiday indulgence or celebrating a significant milestone, you’re coming off a period of being lax in your eating and exercise regiment. This likely resulted in over-eating, and now it’s time to get back on your horse.

To shed those extra pounds, engaging in a caloric deficit is essential. However, caloric deficits naturally trigger hunger, a powerful sensation designed to prevent nutrient withdrawal. For most of us, hunger isn’t life-threatening; it’s a challenge of self-restraint in an environment of abundant food. The key to success lies in how you frame your response to this sensation.

Understanding Hunger

Hunger is often seen as the enemy during weight loss, but it doesn’t have to be. Instead of viewing hunger as a problem, see it as a signal that your body is shifting into fat-burning mode. When you feel hunger during a caloric deficit, remind yourself: "This means it’s working."

Important: If you have extenuating circumstances, underlying health conditions, or are starting from an extremely deconditioned position, consult a doctor before engaging in intentional calorie withdrawal.

Preparing for a Caloric Deficit

Improving your relationship to hunger begins with priming your mindset. The more you practice reframing hunger as a positive signal, the more likely you are to stick to your deficit. Here’s how to prepare:

  • Value self-control: Remind yourself that achieving your goals outweighs the temporary enjoyment of overeating.
  • Focus on the next meal: Hunger is temporary, and relief will come with your next scheduled meal. Stay committed to the plan.
  • Visualize success: Imagine how good it will feel to hit your target weight and achieve your fitness goals.

Additional Tactics

Beyond mental framing, there are practical steps to blunt hunger’s effects:

  • Increase high-protein foods in your diet to promote satiety.
  • Engage in regular exercise to keep your mind and body occupied.
  • Experiment with intermittent fasting or other structured eating patterns to align your meals with hunger cycles.

Step Outside Your Comfort Zone

Like resistance training, a caloric deficit places you outside your comfort zone. The discomfort of hunger is a natural part of the process, but you can use framing to transform it into a signal of progress. With time and discipline, you can learn to embrace hunger as part of your journey toward better health and wellness.
